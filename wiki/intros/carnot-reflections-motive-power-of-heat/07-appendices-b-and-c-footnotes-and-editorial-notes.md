# Section 7: Appendices B and C — Carnot's Footnotes and the Editor's Update

## Appendix B: Carnot's Footnotes

Carnot appended ten substantial footnotes (Notes A–J) to the main text; the longer ones were removed from the body and placed in Appendix B for typographic convenience. They reveal additional depth of reasoning.

### Note A: On perpetual motion and its impossibility

Carnot addresses the possible objection that while mechanical perpetual motion is impossible, heat or electricity might circumvent the prohibition. His response: "should we not suspect that phenomena of heat and electricity are due to some kind of motion of the body, and as such be subjected to the general laws of mechanics?" He also provides a broad definition of what would constitute a genuine perpetual motion: not merely a machine that runs forever after one impulse, but "an apparatus capable of creating motive power in unlimited quantity, capable of starting from rest all the bodies of nature ... capable, finally, of finding in itself the forces necessary to move the whole universe." No such device has ever been built; Voltaic piles claimed to approach this but always show deterioration. This is a careful defence of the foundational impossibility assumption.

### Note B: Evidence for adiabatic temperature change

Carnot lists four experimental proofs that gases change temperature upon compression or rarefaction — the fact on which the whole cycle argument rests:

1. A thermometer under the bell-jar of an air-pump falls 40°–50° when the vacuum is formed; the mist that forms is due to condensation of water vapour from the cooling.
2. Pneumatic tinder-boxes: rapid compression of air to $\frac{1}{8}$–$\frac{1}{10}$ of its volume ignites German tinder.
3. Thermometer placed in a space where compressed air is suddenly released registers a drop.
4. Laplace's correction to Newton's formula for the speed of sound requires assuming that compressions in sound waves are adiabatic (instantaneous), not isothermal. Without this, the predicted speed is $\sqrt{\gamma}$ times too low.

He also addresses a confusing experiment of Gay-Lussac and Welter, where a thermometer placed in a jet of escaping compressed air showed no temperature change. He offers two explanations: (1) the friction of air against the orifice re-heats it, and (2) the air, forced to detour around the thermometer bulb, re-compresses locally. This anticipates the later understanding of throttling processes (the Joule–Thomson effect).

### Notes G and H: Steam expansion and compound engines

Note G credits M. Clément with first clearly developing the principle of steam expansion and demonstrating it applied to existing engines (in an unpublished memoir). Carnot notes the paradox that Mr. Perkins built a 35-atmosphere engine that uses *no* expansion — completely contradicting the principle — and predicts it will not achieve its claimed economy. Note H explains the mechanical advantage of double cylinders: the first (small, high-pressure) produces the main driving force; the second (large, low-pressure) captures the expansion work that would otherwise be wasted. The non-uniform pressure distribution in a single-cylinder engine requires all parts to be designed for maximum load; compound engines distribute the work more evenly.

### Note I: Air engines — the Niepce pyreolophore

Carnot describes the *pyreolophore* of the Niepce brothers: a cylinder into which lycopodium powder (a fine plant spore, used in theatre to produce flash effects) was injected, ignited, and the resulting explosion drove a piston. This is a genuine internal combustion engine, predating the gas engine. Carnot correctly diagnoses its fatal flaw: lycopodium was too expensive. He proposes an alternative design — compressed air passed through a closed furnace containing finely divided fuel — that reads like a description of a continuous-combustion gas turbine. He notes that "there have been made, it is said, recently in England, successful attempts to develop motive power through the action of heat on atmospheric air," but lacks details.

### Note J: Best results from Cornish engines

The record cited in the main text — 65 million pounds of water raised one English foot per bushel of coal — came from an engine at Wheal Abraham, Cornwall, with a 45-inch cylinder and 7-foot stroke. Carnot notes this was exceptional (lasting only one month). Normally 30 million lbs/ft per bushel (about 104,000 kg·m per kg of coal) was considered excellent. A typical ten-horse-power engine would consume about 26 kg of coal per hour to achieve this standard — consistent with contemporary practice.

## Appendix C: The Editor's Update (Thurston, 1897)

Thurston's brief note updates the practical figures from 1824 to 1897:

- Best achievable duty has risen to 150 million ft-lb per 100 lbs of good coal — far above Carnot's best Cornish example.
- Two-thirds of that figure is "extremely common" — reflecting the 70-year improvement in steam-engine technology.
- **Hot-air engines have been superseded by gas engines and oil-vapour engines**, which Thurston warns may "ultimately displace the steam-engine."
- **Compound and triple-expansion engines** are universal where fuel is expensive. The real reason for their success, Thurston explains, is not merely the pressure distribution Carnot cited in Note H: it is the reduction of *cylinder condensation* — thermal cycling of the cylinder walls that in a simple engine wastes 25–50% of the supplied heat. Multiple cylinders at progressively lower temperatures greatly reduce this loss.

Thurston's update frames the 1897 state of the art and points readers to Rankine's and Clausius's 1850–1860 papers and his own *Manual of the Steam-engine* for the full modern applied thermodynamics.

## Significance of the Appendices

Together the appendices show that the *Réflexions* was not a finished treatise but the visible tip of a much larger research programme. The footnotes indicate Carnot's deep familiarity with contemporary experimental literature; the editorial update shows how rapidly the practical realisation of his principles advanced in the seven decades after publication. Read together, they form a compact history of heat-engine science from 1824 to 1897, with Carnot's theory as the organising framework throughout.
