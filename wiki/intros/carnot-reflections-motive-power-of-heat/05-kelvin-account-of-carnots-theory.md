# Section 5: Kelvin's Account of Carnot's Theory (1849)

## Historical context

Sir William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) published this paper in the *Transactions of the Royal Society of Edinburgh* in 1849, when he was 25 years old. It is the paper that rescued Carnot from obscurity and placed his work in the modern context. Kelvin had access to Regnault's monumental experimental work on steam (published in Paris in the 1840s), providing far more accurate data than any available to Carnot. The paper also carefully notes the tension between Carnot's caloric axiom and Joule's recent discovery that heat can be generated by work — a tension Kelvin leaves temporarily unresolved by treating Carnot's axiom as "the most probable basis" pending further data.

## Kelvin's reformulation of Carnot's theory

### The two questions

Kelvin frames the entire theory around two questions:

1. What is the precise *nature* of the thermal agency by which mechanical effect is produced?
2. How may the *amount* of this thermal agency necessary for a given quantity of work be estimated?

**Answer to Q1**: The thermal agency is the *transference of heat from a body at one temperature to a body at a lower temperature*. This is the abstract statement of what every heat-engine does.

**Answer to Q2**: Requires the concept of a *perfect engine* and a numerical coefficient $\mu$.

### The perfect engine criterion

Kelvin restates Carnot's reversibility criterion as:

> **A perfect thermodynamic engine is such that, whatever amount of mechanical effect it can derive from a certain thermal agency, if an equal amount be spent in working it backwards, an equal reverse thermal effect will be produced.**

This is both the definition of maximum efficiency and the proof of its uniqueness: any two perfect engines operating between the same temperatures must produce the same mechanical effect from the same thermal agency, or one could drive the other backwards and the combination would violate the impossibility of perpetual motion.

## The steam-engine cycle (analytical treatment)

Kelvin describes the ideal steam-engine cycle in four operations with a cylinder, piston, and two reservoirs $A$ (at temperature $S$) and $B$ (at temperature $T < S$):

1. Isothermal expansion at temperature $S$: piston rises from $EF$ to $E_1F_1$; heat $H$ absorbed from $A$.
2. Adiabatic expansion from $S$ to $T$: piston rises to $E_2F_2$.
3. Isothermal compression at temperature $T$: piston descends to $E_3F_3$; heat returned to $B$.
4. Adiabatic compression from $T$ to $S$: piston returns to original position.

Using Clapeyron's $p$–$V$ diagram, the net work equals the area of the closed quadrilateral $AA_1A_2A_3$. Kelvin derives analytically:

$$M = H \int_T^S (1-\sigma)\frac{dp/dt}{k}\, dt \tag{1}$$

where $\sigma$ is the ratio of steam density to water density (so $1-\sigma \approx 1$), $dp/dt$ is the rate of change of saturation pressure with temperature, and $k$ is the latent heat per unit volume of steam.

For small temperature intervals ($S - T \ll T$):

$$M = (1-\sigma)\frac{dp/dt}{k} \cdot H(S-T) \tag{2}$$

## The air-engine cycle and Carnot's coefficient $\mu$

For the ideal air engine, using ideal-gas laws (Boyle, Dalton–Gay-Lussac), Kelvin derives:

$$M = \frac{Ep_0 v_0}{v\,dq/dv} \cdot H\tau \tag{3}$$

where $E = 0.00366$ (the volumetric expansion coefficient of ideal gases per °C), $p_0 v_0$ is the pressure–volume product at 0°C, and $dq/dv$ is the heat absorbed per unit volume expansion.

Since perfect steam and air engines produce the same $M$, equating (2) and (3) gives:

$$\mu \equiv (1-\sigma)\frac{dp}{k\,dt} = \frac{Ep_0 v_0}{v\,dq/dv} \tag{6}$$

This **Carnot coefficient $\mu$** is a universal function of temperature alone, measurable from either steam or gas experiments. It has units of mechanical effect per unit of heat per degree of temperature fall:

$$M = H \int_T^S \mu\, dt \tag{7}$$

## Carnot's coefficient from Regnault's data (Table I)

Using Regnault's tables of saturation pressure vs. temperature and latent heat vs. temperature, Kelvin computes $\mu$ for every degree from 0° to 230°C. Key values:

| Temperature (°C) | $\mu$ (ft-lb per thermic unit per °C) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4.960 |
| 50 | 4.343 |
| 100 | 3.837 |
| 150 | 3.490 |
| 200 | 3.249 |
| 230 | 3.137 |

$\mu$ decreases monotonically with temperature — confirming Carnot's qualitative result. Table II gives the cumulative integral $\int_0^t \mu\, dt'$, enabling the net work from any temperature interval to be read off directly.

## Verification and applications

### Comparison with Carnot and Clapeyron

Kelvin tabulates the values of $\mu$ computed by Carnot (from air data) and by Clapeyron (from ether, alcohol, water, turpentine at their boiling points) against his own values from Regnault. The agreement is good except for Carnot's air datum at 0.5°C (4.377 vs. 4.960), which Kelvin attributes to inaccurate 1824 specific-heat data.

### Heat of compression of gases (Joule's experiments)

Kelvin derives that the work needed to compress a gas isothermally from $V$ to $V'$ and the heat evolved are related by:

$$Q = \frac{Ep_0 v_0}{\mu} \ln\frac{V}{V'}, \qquad W = p_0 v_0(1+Et)\ln\frac{V}{V'}$$

$$\frac{W}{Q} = \frac{\mu(1+Et)}{E}$$

At 10°C, this predicts $W/Q \approx 1369$–1379 ft-lb per thermic unit Centigrade. Joule's experiments (1845) gave 1368 for the smallest (and most carefully controlled) series — nearly perfect agreement. This is one of the earliest verifications of Carnot's framework by an independent experiment.

### Economy of real steam engines

Kelvin computes the theoretical duty (work per unit of heat) for boiler temperature 140°C, condenser 30°C: 440 ft-lb per thermic unit. He then benchmarks real engines:

| Engine | Work per unit heat (ft-lb) | % of theoretical duty |
|---|---|---|
| Fowey Consols (Cornish, 1845) | 253 | 57.5% |
| Taylor's engine, United Mines (1840) | 198 | 45.1% |
| French engines (contracted) | 159 | 36.1% |
| English engines (contracted) | 131 | 29.7% |
| Average Cornish engines | 111 | 25.3% |
| Common Lancashire engines | 38 | 8.6% |

The best engines of 1849 achieved about 57% of the Carnot limit. This framing — actual efficiency as a percentage of Carnot efficiency — remains the standard way to assess heat-engine performance today.

## Significance

Kelvin's paper is the bridge between Carnot's qualitative 1824 framework and the quantitative science of thermodynamics. It introduces the notation $\mu$ (Carnot's coefficient), shows how to compute it empirically, defines the concept of thermodynamic efficiency as a fraction of the Carnot limit, and frames the comparison of steam vs. air engines in terms of the fall of caloric utilised. Within two years, Kelvin and Clausius would use this framework to formulate the [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]] in its modern form and define [[entropy]].
