# Section 6: Appendix A — Extracts from Carnot's Unpublished Writings

## Overview

Appendix A, compiled by Hyppolyte Carnot from the working notebooks that survived (most papers were burned as cholera-precaution), reveals a private Carnot quite different from the public one. While the *Réflexions* works within the caloric theory, the notebooks show that by ca. 1830–1832 Carnot had privately abandoned it in favour of a dynamical theory of heat — arriving at the First Law of Thermodynamics independently of Mayer (1842) and Joule (1843–49).

## Personal maxims and character

The notebooks open with personal conduct rules that illuminate Carnot's intellectual method: "Plan in the morning the work of the day, reflect in the evening on what has been done." He lists physical and intellectual exercises to alternate: dancing, fencing, swimming, skating, turning, joinery, gardening, reading, music, versification. He advocates eight hours of sleep and great sobriety. These are not incidental — they reflect a mind that understood the importance of deliberate, disciplined variation in mental work.

His moral aphorisms are equally revealing: "Why not say more frequently, 'I do not know'?", "Speak little of what you know, and not at all of what you do not know", "How much modesty adds to merit! A man of talent who conceals his knowledge is like a branch bending under a weight of fruit." These maxims explain the restraint in the *Réflexions*, where Carnot repeatedly qualifies results and declines to over-claim.

## The private rejection of caloric theory

In the scientific notes Carnot states bluntly:

> "When a hypothesis no longer suffices to explain phenomena, it should be abandoned. This is the case with the hypothesis which regards caloric as matter, as a subtle fluid."

He then lists the experimental evidence against caloric:

1. **Heat from percussion and friction** (Rumford): heat appears without any source body being cooled, and at the point of friction specifically.
2. **Air-pump experiments**: compressing air raises its temperature in a way inconsistent with a simple redistribution of caloric.
3. **Free expansion**: when air rushes into a vacuum, temperature changes occur that cannot be explained by caloric redistribution.
4. **Radiating heat as vibratory motion**: "Light is generally regarded as the result of a vibratory movement of the ethereal fluid. Light produces heat ... Could a motion (that of radiating heat) produce matter (caloric)? No, undoubtedly; it can only produce a motion. Heat is then the result of a motion."

## The First Law in Carnot's words

The notebooks contain a passage that is a direct statement of the [[first-law-of-thermodynamics]]:

> "Heat is simply motive power, or rather motion which has changed form. It is a movement among the particles of bodies. Wherever there is destruction of motive power there is, at the same time, production of heat in quantity exactly proportional to the quantity of motive power destroyed. Reciprocally, wherever there is destruction of heat, there is production of motive power."

And the conservation of energy:

> "We can then establish the general proposition that motive power is, in quantity, invariable in nature; that it is, correctly speaking, never either produced or destroyed. It is true that it changes form, that is, it produces sometimes one sort of motion, sometimes another, but it is never annihilated."

This is a complete statement of energy conservation applied to heat and mechanical work, written at least 10 years before Mayer published it and at least 15 years before Joule's definitive experimental confirmation.

## The mechanical equivalent of heat — Carnot's estimate

Carnot gives a quantitative estimate:

> "According to some ideas that I have formed on the theory of heat, the production of a unit of motive power necessitates the destruction of 2.70 units of heat."

His unit of motive power (a "dyname") = 1 cubic metre of water raised 1 metre = 1000 kilogram-metres. His unit of heat is therefore $\frac{1000}{2.70} \approx 370$ kilogram-metres per kilocalorie. The modern value (from $J = 4186$ J/kcal) is approximately $427$ kilogram-metres per kcal. Carnot's estimate is within 13% — better than Mayer's 1842 value of 365 km/kcal derived from the same qualitative data.

## Planned experiments

The notebooks contain an extensive list of experiments Carnot intended to perform — experiments that would have constituted a full empirical programme for the dynamical theory:

- Repeat Rumford's friction-in-water experiment while measuring both motive power consumed and heat produced.
- Strike pieces of lead and other metals; measure motive power consumed and temperature rise.
- Agitate water or mercury in a pump; measure motive power consumed and heat produced.
- Admit air into a vacuum and measure temperature changes (anticipating Joule's free-expansion experiment).
- Measure vapour tensions of water, alcohol, ether, mercury, and sulphide of carbon at a wide range of temperatures.

These are precisely the experiments Joule performed in Manchester in the 1840s. Had Carnot lived (he died at 36 in 1832), he would almost certainly have established the mechanical equivalent of heat experimentally and unified the theories of heat and work before mid-century.

## Significance

Appendix A transforms the *Réflexions* from a historically important but flawed document (built on the caloric theory) into something far more remarkable: evidence that Carnot was, by 1832, in simultaneous possession of the embryonic forms of both the First and Second Laws of Thermodynamics. His public work gives the Second Law (maximum efficiency of heat-engines); his private notes give the First (conservation of energy including heat). The tragedy is that neither was known to the world until decades later, when Kelvin and Clausius had to reconstruct them independently.
