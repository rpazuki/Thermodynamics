# Section 0: Editorial Preface and Biography of Sadi Carnot

## The editor's introduction (R. H. Thurston)

Thurston opens by calling Carnot "perhaps the greatest genius, in the department of physical science at least, that this century has produced" — a judgment justified by Carnot's having produced his sole published work at age 23–24, work that founded a new science while its author accepted the dominant (incorrect) caloric theory only provisionally. Thurston places Carnot in the company of Euclid, Newton, and Lavoisier as exemplars of genius that is "ready at the earliest maturity."

### Historical context: the caloric controversy

In 1824 the scientific community was split on the nature of heat. The dominant view, following Newton and Lavoisier, held that heat is an imponderable substance ([[caloric]]) that flows between bodies. A minority, following Rumford's cannon-boring experiments (1798) and Davy's ice-melting experiments, argued that heat is "a mode of motion." Carnot worked formally within the caloric framework — his cycle conserves caloric — yet privately doubted it. His unpublished notes (see [[carnot-reflections-motive-power-of-heat/appendix-a-unpublished-writings]]) show he had essentially reached the correct dynamical view before 1832.

### Carnot's principal discoveries as summarised by Thurston

Thurston enumerates the claims in Carnot's text that were subsequently rediscovered and credited to others:

1. The maximum motive power from heat is determined solely by the temperature range, independent of the working substance.
2. Efficiency increases with the temperature range; high-pressure engines are superior because they exploit a larger temperature fall.
3. The fall of caloric produces more motive power at lower than at higher temperatures (i.e., $\mu(t)$ is a decreasing function of $t$).
4. The difference between $c_p$ and $c_v$ is the same for all gases — a result disputed until Clausius confirmed it via Regnault's data.
5. Heat absorbed by a gas during isothermal expansion is the same for all gases at the same temperature and volume ratio.
6. Carnot estimated the mechanical equivalent of heat as 2.70 dynames (cubic-metre-of-water raised one metre) per unit of heat, equivalent to $\approx 370$ kilogram-metres per kcal. This is close to Mayer's 1842 value of 365 km/kcal.

Thurston emphasises the irony: Carnot's book was published in 1824, sold out its small print-run, and was largely forgotten until Clapeyron (1834) illustrated it with the $p$–$V$ diagram, and Kelvin (1849) rescued and corrected it.

## Life of Sadi Carnot (by Hyppolyte Carnot, his brother)

Nicolas-Léonard-Sadi Carnot was born 1 June 1796 in the Palais du Luxembourg, Paris, the son of Lazare Carnot ("the Organiser of Victory"), Director of the Republic. Named after the Persian poet Sa'di. He entered the École Polytechnique at 16, graduating first in artillery (1813).

### Military career and its frustrations

After the Napoleonic defeat Sadi, bearing the politically disfavoured Carnot name, was assigned to minor garrison postings — counting bricks and drawing plans. In 1819 he transferred to the general staff, then obtained indefinite leave to study in Paris. He visited workshops, followed courses at the Collège de France, the Sorbonne, the École des Mines, and the Bibliothèque — a pattern of broad self-directed learning that infuses every page of the *Réflexions*.

### The *Réflexions* (1824) and its reception

Hyppolyte recalls that Sadi, anxious to be perfectly clear, made him read passages aloud to test comprehension. The book was published in 1824 and barely noticed. Sadi returned to study, planning experiments on the specific heats of gases and vapours. The Revolution of July 1830 briefly engaged him politically; then he returned to physics. His health collapsed in mid-1832: inflammation of the lungs, scarlet fever, brain fever. He died 24 August 1832, aged 36, from cholera — all his papers burned as a cholera-precaution, surviving only in those Hyppolyte had already copied.

### Character

Hyppolyte's portrait reveals a man of intense privacy, wide curiosity (gymnastics, fencing, swimming, music, political economy), and moral seriousness. Sadi's personal notebooks contain maxims on conduct, observations on political economy, sceptical religious reflections, and — most importantly for science — working notes that show his private evolution towards the dynamical theory of heat.

## Significance for the history of thermodynamics

The biographical section establishes two crucial facts. First, Carnot had essentially anticipated the First Law — the conservation of energy as it applies to heat — at least eight years before Mayer and Joule. Second, the premature death at 36 and the destruction of most papers robbed science of what might have been a complete thermodynamics a generation earlier. The *Réflexions* itself survived only in the hundred-odd copies of the original printing, rescued by Clapeyron and then by Kelvin.
