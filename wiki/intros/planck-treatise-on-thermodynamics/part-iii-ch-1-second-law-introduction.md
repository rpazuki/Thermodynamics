# Part III, Chapter I: The Second Law — Introduction (§§106–115)

*Source: [[planck-treatise-on-thermodynamics]]*

---

## Overview

This chapter introduces the Second Law of Thermodynamics as a fundamentally new principle — one that the First Law cannot provide. It explains *why* we need a second law (direction of processes), identifies specific irreversible processes that define its content, introduces the concepts of **reversible** and **irreversible** processes, and prepares the reader for the formal proof in Chapter II. This is one of the most philosophically careful passages in the book.

---

## 1. What the First Law Cannot Tell Us (§§106–108)

The First Law constrains *how much* energy is exchanged in any process, but says nothing about *which direction* a process will spontaneously proceed. Three concrete examples:

1. **Heat conduction:** Two bodies at different temperatures exchange heat. The First Law only requires that the total heat is conserved; it does not say whether heat flows from hot to cold or cold to hot.
2. **Chemical reactions:** Equation (50), $\{\mathrm{H_2}\} + \tfrac{1}{2}\{\mathrm{O_2}\} - (\mathrm{H_2O}) = 68{,}400\,\mathrm{cal}$, is symmetric — it says nothing about whether hydrogen and oxygen combine to form water, or water decomposes, or neither. The First Law treats initial and final states as completely equivalent.
3. **Mechanical direction:** If a system has a particular form of energy at an absolute minimum, the First Law prescribes that this energy must increase on any change — giving a directional criterion. But in thermal phenomena, no such universal minimum has been identified, so this analogy fails.

Some authors attempted to reduce the Second Law to "heat cannot be completely converted to work." Planck argues (§108) this is too special: a perfect gas expanding isothermally while in contact with a heat reservoir *does* convert heat completely to work without any compensating change. The statement is not general enough to serve as the Second Law.

---

## 2. Three Paradigmatic Irreversible Processes (§§109–111)

Planck introduces the Second Law through three specific experimental facts, each claiming that a certain process **cannot be completely reversed** — meaning no combination of mechanical, thermal, chemical, or electrical processes can restore the exact initial state of *everything*:

**Proposition 1 — Frictional heat generation (§109):** When falling weights generate heat in water by friction (Joule's experiment), no process can completely reverse this — raising the weights back, cooling the liquid, and leaving nothing else changed. This is the paradigm case of irreversibility.

**Proposition 2 — Free gas expansion (§110):** When a gas expands into a vacuum (no external work, no heat exchange), no process can return the gas to its original volume without leaving some change elsewhere. Suppose it could: one could then allow the gas to expand again while in contact with a heat reservoir (converting heat to work), then reverse the expansion — achieving net work from a single reservoir, which is perpetual motion of the second kind.

**Proposition 3 — Heat conduction (§111):** Heat flowing from a hot body to a cold one cannot be fully reversed. The reversal would require transferring heat back uphill (from cold to hot) without any other change, which connects back to Proposition 1 by analysis of the Carnot cycle.

Other irreversible processes include: diffusion, solidification of an overcooled liquid, condensation of a supersaturated vapour, explosive reactions, and any spontaneous process that increases stability.

---

## 3. Definition of Reversible and Irreversible Processes (§112)

> *A process which can in no way be completely reversed is termed **irreversible**; all other processes **reversible**.*

Key subtleties:
- "Completely reversed" means restoring the exact initial conditions of **all** bodies involved — not just the system under study.
- Many processes that cannot be directly reversed are nevertheless reversible (e.g., a pendulum swinging backward, a compressed spring releasing). The full requirement is that *no* combination of processes whatsoever can achieve restoration.

---

## 4. Existence of Reversible Processes (§§113–114)

Reversible processes actually exist in idealisation:
- **Infinitely slow processes** (§71): sequences of equilibrium states that can be directly reversed step by step.
- **Perfectly periodic processes:** pendula, planetary orbits — they return exactly to their initial state after one period.
- **Friction-free mechanical processes:** frictionless pistons, inextensible belts, perfectly rigid connecting rods.

The conceptual issue (§114) is that the decision of whether a process is reversible or irreversible depends only on the **initial and final states**, not on the path. The Second Law will therefore establish a relation between quantities attached to states — precisely what the concept of entropy will accomplish.

Planck makes a striking logical point: it can be proved (§113, proved in §127) that all the processes listed as irreversible in §§109–111 are either *all* reversible or *all* irreversible. There is no middle ground. If even one of them could be completely reversed, all the others could be reversed too. Therefore:
- Either the Second Law (and all its consequences) is universally valid, or
- All of those processes are reversible, in which case the entire edifice collapses.

Every experimental confirmation of any consequence of the Second Law simultaneously supports all other consequences.

---

## 5. All Real Processes Are Irreversible (§115)

Since no real process is entirely free from friction or heat conduction, **all processes that actually occur in nature are irreversible**. Reversible processes are an ideal limiting case — approached as friction and thermal gradients are reduced to zero. Nevertheless, they are the essential theoretical tool for deriving conditions of equilibrium and maximum efficiency, because the Second Law takes its simplest form for reversible processes.

---

## Significance of the Introduction

This chapter is notable for its epistemological clarity. Planck:
1. Shows why the First Law is insufficient (direction).
2. Formulates the Second Law through concrete, falsifiable propositions rather than abstract statements about entropy.
3. Identifies the logical unity of all irreversible processes — any one disproof would disprove all others.
4. Frames reversibility as an ideal limiting case of real processes, not an exact description.

The stage is now set for Chapter II, where Planck formally proves the Second Law (in the form of the entropy theorem) from the empirical proposition that **perpetual motion of the second kind** — a machine that converts heat from a single reservoir entirely into work in a cyclic process — is impossible.
