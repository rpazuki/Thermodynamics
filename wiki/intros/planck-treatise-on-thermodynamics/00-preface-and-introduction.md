# Preface and Translator's Notice

*Source: [[planck-treatise-on-thermodynamics]]*

---

## Translator's Notice (Alexander Ogg, 1903)

Alexander Ogg's brief notice explains that the English translation was motivated by the growing importance of thermodynamics for physical chemistry. Ogg adopted a notation change: he uses $\Psi$ for what he calls the *Planck'sches Potential* — the thermodynamic potential of Gibbs and Duhem divided by $-\theta$. This function, which Planck himself later calls $\Psi = \Phi - (U+pV)/\theta$, becomes the master equilibrium function for the entire fourth part of the book. The translator also acknowledges Planck's co-operation in adding a few notes and in approving symbol changes to bring the work into conformity with English conventions.

## Planck's Preface (Berlin, April 1897)

### Motivation and Scope

Planck wrote the *Treatise* in response to repeated requests to either collect his published thermodynamics papers or to synthesise them into a comprehensive text. He chose the latter, expressly aiming to present the **entire field of thermodynamics from a uniform point of view**. He describes the work as an *introductory textbook* for students already familiar with elementary physics, chemistry, and calculus — not an original research monograph. He notes, however, that his earlier papers sometimes contain more detailed derivations on specific points, and he appends a catalogue of these papers cross-referenced to the book's sections.

### The Three Historical Methods of Thermodynamics

Planck's preface is philosophically rich. He identifies three distinct approaches that had been used to develop thermodynamics up to 1897:

**1. The Kinetic Theory (molecular approach).**  
Founded by Joule, Waterston, Krönig, and Clausius, extended by Maxwell and Boltzmann. This approach treats heat as the kinetic energy of molecular motions. It is potentially the deepest approach but faces, in Planck's words, "obstacles at present unsurmountable" — both the mathematical complexity and fundamental difficulties in mechanically interpreting the laws of thermodynamics (he alludes to what would later crystallise in the Boltzmann $H$-theorem controversy and Loschmidt's reversibility objection).

**2. Helmholtz's Mechanical Theory (constrained hypothesis).**  
This approach accepts only the hypothesis that heat is *some kind* of motion, without specifying its character. It is more philosophically secure than the full kinetic theory but does not offer a sufficiently broad foundation from which new results can be derived; it mainly verifies known laws.

**3. The Empirical–Phenomenological Approach (Planck's own method).**  
This is the method used exclusively in the book. It starts from **two very general empirical facts** — the two fundamental laws of thermodynamics — and, by pure logical deduction, derives a large body of physical and chemical laws that have been verified without exception. The approach deliberately sets aside all questions about the ultimate nature of heat. Planck acknowledges this cannot be the final word: the aspiration for a *unified theory of nature* on mechanical or electromagnetic grounds will eventually subsume thermodynamics. But as of 1897, no such unification is possible, and the phenomenological method is the most fruitful available.

### Philosophical Stance on Reductionism

Planck closes with a remarkably balanced statement: even if a more general mechanical or electromagnetic theory is eventually found, the results deduced from the two laws of thermodynamics would not be *invalidated* — they would simply be rederived as consequences of the more fundamental theory. The autonomy of thermodynamic results is thus assured regardless of the ultimate fate of the phenomenological method. This stance prefigures the modern view of thermodynamics as an *effective theory* valid at macroscopic scales irrespective of microscopic details.

## Key Methodological Principles Announced in the Preface

| Principle | Implication for the book |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Empirical foundation only | No molecular hypotheses anywhere in the main text |
| Uniform viewpoint | Every result derived from the same two postulates |
| Introductory character | Worked numerical examples; cross-references to tables |
| Awareness of limits | Acknowledges kinetic theory may eventually supersede phenomenological approach |

---

## Significance for Reading the Book

The preface is essential for understanding Planck's logical structure. Every time the book introduces a new result — whether about gas mixtures, chemical equilibria, or osmotic pressure — the derivation is traceable back to:
1. The impossibility of perpetual motion of the first kind (First Law).
2. The impossibility of perpetual motion of the second kind (Second Law).

No molecular dynamics, statistical mechanics, or microscopic model is ever invoked. This makes the book simultaneously more mathematically rigorous and more philosophically cautious than contemporaneous treatments.
