## Summary

This 1865 paper by Rudolf Clausius is the landmark text in which he formally names [[entropy]] and states the two laws of [[thermodynamics]] in their most celebrated aphoristic form. Clausius begins by compactly restating both laws of the mechanical heat theory: the equivalence of heat and work (first law), and the equivalence of transformations (second law). He shows that for any cyclic process the integral $\oint dQ/T \leq 0$, with equality holding only for fully reversible cycles. He then argues that for reversible processes this integrand must be the complete differential of a state function, which he defines and names the *entropy* $S$, deriving the relation $dS = dQ/T$. The paper closes with a sweeping cosmological statement: the energy of the universe is constant, and the entropy of the universe tends toward a maximum.

## Key claims

- The first law is expressed as $dQ = dU + dw$, where $U$ is internal energy and $dw$ is external work in heat units (source: Clausius1865.pdf).
- For any cyclic process, $\oint dQ/T \leq 0$; the equality holds if and only if every step is reversible (source: Clausius1865.pdf).
- Because $\oint dQ/T = 0$ for all reversible cycles, $dQ/T$ is an exact differential of a state function. Clausius designates this function $S$ and names it the **entropy** of the body, deriving $dS = dQ/T$ (source: Clausius1865.pdf).
- The word *entropy* is taken deliberately from the Greek ἡ τροπή ("the transformation") so that it parallels *energy* and can be used unchanged across modern languages (source: Clausius1865.pdf).
- Entropy is the sum of two contributions: the *transformation value of the heat content* and the *disgregation* (a measure of molecular arrangement) (source: Clausius1865.pdf).
- Applied to the universe, the two laws yield: (1) the energy of the universe is constant; (2) the entropy of the universe strives toward a maximum (source: Clausius1865.pdf).
- Transformations in nature proceed spontaneously only in the positive sense; they can proceed in the negative sense only when compensated by simultaneous positive transformations (source: Clausius1865.pdf).

## Methods

The paper is primarily analytical. Clausius works within his prior framework of the mechanical heat theory, using cyclic-process integrals to establish the properties of $dQ/T$. He exploits the fact that a cyclic integral that always vanishes implies the integrand is a perfect differential — a classical argument in the calculus of differentials. No experiments are described; the paper synthesises and reformulates results established in his earlier papers (notably 1850 and 1854), recasting them in the most compact and general notation.

## Limitations

- The paper is an excerpt and translation; the full original (pp. 353–400) contains more detailed calculations not reproduced here.
- Clausius acknowledges that the full treatment must be extended to *radiant heat* (electromagnetic radiation in the ether) and to the motions of ponderable masses, but defers this to future work.
- The concept of *disgregation* as a component of entropy was later abandoned; modern thermodynamics absorbs it into statistical entropy without the separate term.
- No explicit statistical or molecular derivation of entropy is given; that would come from [[gibbs-elementary-principles-statistical-mechanics]] and Boltzmann's subsequent work.

## Connections

- Directly introduces [[entropy]] as a named state function, making this one of the most consequential terminological and conceptual acts in the history of [[thermodynamics]].
- The inequality $\oint dQ/T \leq 0$ generalises the result Clausius had derived from [[carnot-cycle]] analysis, now freed from any reference to a specific engine.
- The statement that entropy of the universe tends to a maximum is the seed of the [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]] in its modern cosmological form; Clausius credits W. Thomson (Lord Kelvin) for first drawing attention to this implication.
- [[planck-treatise-on-thermodynamics]] later systematised and extended Clausius's framework, building on the entropy concept defined here.
- Carathéodory's axiomatic programme (see [[redlich-fundamental-thermodynamics-since-caratheodory]] and [[pogliani-caratheodory-axiomatic-thermodynamics]]) is in large part an attempt to derive entropy without the cyclic-machine machinery that Clausius uses here.

## Quotes

> "By designating this quantity as S, we can write $dS = dQ/T$… In searching for a significant name for S, we could say, similar to what we said of U being the heat and work content of the body, that S should be the transformation content of the body. However, I believe it is better to take the names of such scientifically important units from the classical languages so that they can be used unchanged in all modern languages; therefore I propose to designate S by the Greek word ἡ τροπή = the transformation, the **entropy** of the body." (source: Clausius1865.pdf)

> "The second law, in the form which I have given it, states the fact that all transformations which occur in nature occur in a certain sense which I have taken as positive, of themselves, that is, without compensation, but that they can only occur in the opposite or negative sense in such a way that they are compensated by positive transformations which occur at the same time." (source: Clausius1865.pdf)

> "1. The energy of the universe is constant. 2. The entropy of the universe strives toward a maximum." (source: Clausius1865.pdf)
