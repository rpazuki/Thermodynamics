## Summary

Redlich provides a critical intellectual survey of axiomatic [[thermodynamics]] from Carathéodory's 1909 paper through the mid-1960s, arguing that unresolved confusion in basic concepts — particularly the conflation of *generalized coordinates and forces* with *extensive and intensive properties* — is the root cause of widespread unease in the field. He summarises Carathéodory's reformulation of both the first law (via adiabatic walls) and the second law (via the inaccessibility principle and Pfaffian theory), and then reviews subsequent contributions by Born, Buchdahl, Ehrenfest-Afanassjewa, Landsberg, Falk, and others. Redlich concludes by proposing his own framework for basic concepts — object, isolation, interaction, generalised coordinates and forces — grounded in the Kantian idea that fundamental concepts are those that are indispensable for describing all observations. The overarching thesis is that axiomatics in thermodynamics has remained sterile because its practitioners took basic physical concepts for granted rather than analysing them critically.

## Key claims

- Carathéodory's most important contribution to the **first law** was eliminating heat as a primary observable concept, defining it derivatively via $Q = E_f - E_i - W$ once energy is defined through adiabatic work (source: RevModPhys.40.556.pdf).
- The **second law** in Carathéodory's system follows from the empirical principle that in the neighbourhood of any state there exist states adiabatically inaccessible from it; this leads, via Pfaffian theory, to the existence of entropy $S$ and absolute temperature $T$ as state functions satisfying $dS = dQ/T$ (source: RevModPhys.40.556.pdf).
- Caratheodory's treatment was largely ignored until Born (1921) published a simplified and enthusiastic presentation; after that it attracted Landsberg, Ehrenfest-Afanassjewa, Falk, Callen, and others (source: RevModPhys.40.556.pdf).
- Buchdahl (1958) offered a superior formulation of the inaccessibility principle by classifying states into three classes relative to a reference state: adiabatic-reversibly accessible, adiabatic-irreversibly accessible, and adiabatically inaccessible — a three-fold partition that directly furnishes a Dedekind cut and hence entropy (source: RevModPhys.40.556.pdf).
- The "zeroth law" is not an empirical law but a *rule of order* imposed on descriptions of nature; its apparent failures (e.g. neutron-permeable vs. neutron-absorbing objects in thermal equilibrium) signal the existence of a new, previously unrecognised mode of interaction rather than a breakdown of temperature (source: RevModPhys.40.556.pdf).
- **Generalised forces** (pressure, voltage, surface tension, chemical potential, temperature) possess a unique epistemological property: they can be measured only by establishing equilibrium between the object and a gauge (source: RevModPhys.40.556.pdf).
- Subsequent axiomatists — including Falk and Jung — confused generalised coordinates and forces with the extensive/intensive distinction, producing a fundamental terminological and conceptual error (source: RevModPhys.40.556.pdf).
- Carathéodory unduly restricted his treatment to gaseous and liquid phases (by analogy with Gibbs, but without Gibbs's justification of intentional scope restriction), thereby limiting its generality from the outset (source: RevModPhys.40.556.pdf).

## Methods

The paper is a critical literature review combined with a constructive conceptual proposal. Redlich surveys the primary sources directly (Carathéodory 1909, Born 1921, Buchdahl 1949–1958, Ehrenfest-Afanassjewa 1925, Landsberg 1956/1961, Falk and Jung 1959, Callen 1960) and offers counter-examples and logical analyses of their definitions. The constructive Sections 4–5 introduce basic concepts operationally, following a Popperian criterion of refutability: a concept is admissible only if an experimental procedure decides its value. The Dedekind-cut framework is used throughout to define measurable properties.

## Limitations

- Redlich's positive proposal (Sections 4–5) is sketched rather than fully developed; he refers readers to his earlier 1962 paper (*J. Phys. Chem.* 66, 585) for details.
- The review is primarily conceptual and historical; it does not develop new mathematical results.
- Redlich is notably dismissive of the entire post-Carathéodory axiomatic programme, which may understate genuine mathematical contributions (e.g. Landsberg's 1956 *Rev. Mod. Phys.* paper).
- The paper predates major later work on the foundations of thermodynamics (e.g. Lieb and Yngvason's 1999 rigorous entropy derivation).

## Connections

- The Carathéodory inaccessibility principle and Pfaffian treatment of [[entropy]] discussed here are explained in greater pedagogical depth in [[pogliani-caratheodory-axiomatic-thermodynamics]].
- Redlich explicitly traces the tradition back to [[planck-treatise-on-thermodynamics]] (Planck 1897), noting that Planck gave "the most beautiful realisation" of the pre-Carathéodory axiomatic programme.
- Clausius's original definition $dS = dQ/T$ (introduced in [[clausius-1865-main-equations]]) is the historical anchor against which Carathéodory's adiabatic-inaccessibility route to entropy is contrasted.
- The generalised-forces framework proposed in Section 4 connects to concepts of [[thermodynamics]] well beyond classical pressure-volume work, foreshadowing modern treatments of chemical potential, electrochemical cells, and surface thermodynamics.
- Buchdahl's three-class partition of states, highlighted by Redlich as superior, is a key step toward the Lieb-Yngvason entropy axiomatics.
