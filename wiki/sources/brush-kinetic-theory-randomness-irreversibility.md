## Summary

This is the eighth instalment of Brush's monumental history of [[kinetic-theory]], covering the period roughly 1850–1910. It traces how the concepts of randomness and irreversibility were forged out of the collision between Newtonian mechanics and the [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]]. Beginning with the historical context of the "world-machine" and Clausius's coinage of [[entropy]], the paper follows the path from Maxwell's velocity-distribution work and the introduction of probabilistic ideas, through Boltzmann's derivation of the H-theorem and his identification of entropy with molecular disorder, to the Loschmidt reversibility objection and the Zermelo recurrence paradox, and finally to the emergence of the ergodic hypothesis and Planck's work on irreversible radiation. Brush argues that the need for an explicit postulate of *randomness* — later crystallised in Boltzmann's "molecular disorder" assumption — did not emerge clearly from the broad statistical debates of Maxwell and Thomson, but only when Culverwell and Burbury pressed the technical details of the H-theorem proof in the 1890s. The article is remarkable for its archival depth, drawing on unpublished correspondence and recovering forgotten contributors such as Rankine and Burbury.

## Key claims

- The central tension of late-nineteenth-century physics was whether Newtonian reversible mechanics could be reconciled with the irreversibility of the [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]]; this was still an open question at the 1900 Paris International Physics Congress (source: Brush-DevelopmentKineticTheory-1974.pdf).
- Clausius introduced [[entropy]] as a state function in 1865, summarising his two fundamental theorems as "the energy of the universe is constant; the entropy of the universe tends toward a maximum" (source: Brush-DevelopmentKineticTheory-1974.pdf).
- Boltzmann's 1872 paper "Weitere Studien über das Wärmegleichgewicht" (the [[boltzmann-h-theorem]] paper) is described as "one of the most important and influential works in the entire history of kinetic theory"; it implicitly assumed molecular chaos (Stosszahlansatz) without stating it explicitly (source: Brush-DevelopmentKineticTheory-1974.pdf).
- The reversibility paradox: Loschmidt (1876) pointed out that for every trajectory satisfying the H-theorem there is a time-reversed trajectory along which H *increases*, so the H-theorem cannot be a purely mechanical result; Boltzmann replied with a probabilistic argument that most initial conditions lead to increasing entropy, but this required an implicit assumption about the randomness of initial data (source: Brush-DevelopmentKineticTheory-1974.pdf).
- Burbury (1890–1894) introduced the letter "H" for Boltzmann's functional (which Boltzmann had called "E") and was the first to state explicitly that the proof of the H-theorem requires an extraneous probabilistic postulate — that molecular coordinates are "taken at haphazard" — now called the Stosszahlansatz or molecular chaos assumption (source: Brush-DevelopmentKineticTheory-1974.pdf).
- The Zermelo recurrence paradox (1896), based on Poincaré's recurrence theorem, showed that any finite mechanical system must eventually return arbitrarily close to its initial state, apparently contradicting perpetual entropy increase; Boltzmann replied that recurrence times are astronomically long (source: Brush-DevelopmentKineticTheory-1974.pdf).
- Rankine had introduced a "thermodynamic function" equivalent to Clausius's equivalence-value as early as 1854, but his entanglement with the molecular-vortex hypothesis denied him credit; Brush recovers this priority claim (source: Brush-DevelopmentKineticTheory-1974.pdf).
- Planck's work on irreversible radiation processes (§8) is shown to be a further arena in which the tension between mechanics and irreversibility played out, preparing the way for quantum theory (source: Brush-DevelopmentKineticTheory-1974.pdf).

## Methods

Brush uses a combination of primary-source analysis (journal papers, monographs, unpublished correspondence at Cambridge and Oxford) and contextual historiography. He cross-references German, French, and British scientific communities, tracing ideas through citation networks and personal correspondence. The narrative is roughly chronological but thematic, organised around the evolving understanding of randomness rather than strictly by date.

## Limitations

- The article is explicitly restricted to developments before 1900 (with a brief excursion into Planck); later ergodic theory (Birkhoff, von Neumann, 1930s) is touched on only in passing.
- Brush focuses on the German and English-language literature; French contributions (Poincaré excepted) receive less systematic treatment.
- As a history of science paper, it describes the debate but does not adjudicate between interpretations of probability in physics.
- The article predates later historiographical work on Boltzmann by Cercignani, Gallavotti, and others, so some interpretations may need updating.

## Connections

- Central to the history of [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]] and [[entropy]].
- The [[boltzmann-h-theorem]] is the focal technical result; the molecular disorder assumption (Stosszahlansatz) is its contested foundation.
- Gibbs's *Elementary Principles in Statistical Mechanics* ([[gibbs-elementary-principles-statistical-mechanics]]) is discussed as a parallel approach that avoided H-theorem difficulties by working with ensemble averages.
- The reversibility and recurrence paradoxes connect to the ergodic hypothesis and, ultimately, to modern ergodic theory and chaos.
