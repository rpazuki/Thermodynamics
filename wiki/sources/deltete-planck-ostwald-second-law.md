## Summary

This essay reconstructs and evaluates the prolonged intellectual conflict between Max Planck and Wilhelm Ostwald over the proper interpretation of the [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]], drawing extensively on their published correspondence (1891–1898). Deltete argues that Planck's interpretation—the Second Law as the principle of the universal and irreversible increase of [[entropy]]—was correct and rightly prevailed over Ostwald's energetic alternative. For Planck, irreversibility is the *essence* of the Second Law: without the assumption that genuinely irreversible processes exist, the entire edifice of thermodynamics collapses. Ostwald, by contrast, embedded the Second Law within his broader "energetics," treating it as a law confined to the behaviour of heat and insisting that all scientific proofs using the Second Law rely only on its limiting reversible (equality) case, not its irreversibility (inequality). The correspondence shows Planck raising ever more pointed examples (isothermal diffusion, dilution of solutions) to demonstrate irreversibility without heat change, while Ostwald persistently deflects these. Deltete contextualises this within the 1895 Lübeck confrontation between Boltzmann and the energeticists (Helm, Ostwald), after which Planck issued his categorical public rejection of energetics as scientifically worthless.

## Key claims

- Planck held from his 1879 doctoral thesis that the [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]] is most fundamentally the *principle of the increase of entropy*, governing not just heat but all natural processes, and that its essence is the existence of genuinely irreversible processes (source: Deltete-PlanckOstwaldSecond-2012.pdf).
- Ostwald's energetics treated thermodynamics as a sub-chapter of a more general science of energy, reducing the Second Law to the behaviour of heat and its intensity law; he denied that irreversibility or [[entropy]] increase forms the essence or proof of the Second Law (source: Deltete-PlanckOstwaldSecond-2012.pdf).
- Ostwald's key claim—that "all proofs by means of the second law make use of the equality and not of the corresponding inequality"—was sharply contested by Planck, who maintained that the inequality ($dS > 0$) is indispensable to both the proof and all applications of the Second Law (source: Deltete-PlanckOstwaldSecond-2012.pdf).
- Planck's 1893 essay "Der Kern des zweiten Hauptsatzes" stated that the essence of the Second Law is "the assumption that there are irreversible processes," and that if any single such process were found to be reversible, the entire structure of thermodynamics would have to be rebuilt (source: Deltete-PlanckOstwaldSecond-2012.pdf).
- Ostwald's Factorization Principle ($E = ic$, factoring each energy form into intensity and capacity) provided his energetic "derivation" of the Second Law via $Q = Tc$, yielding $dQ = TdS$; but Planck objected that this conflated state functions with path-dependent quantities and failed to account for irreversibility at all (source: Deltete-PlanckOstwaldSecond-2012.pdf).
- The Clausius formulation of the Second Law (1865) defined entropy $S$ by $dS \geq dQ/T$, with equality for reversible processes and strict inequality for irreversible ones; Planck extended this to all natural processes, not just thermal ones (source: Deltete-PlanckOstwaldSecond-2012.pdf).
- Planck's 1896 essay "Gegen die neuere Energetik" constituted a categorical public rejection of Ostwald's energetics, which he saw as obscuring the fundamental distinction between reversible and irreversible processes and thereby rendering "meaningless" the concepts and propositions required for thermodynamic applications to chemistry and electrochemistry (source: Deltete-PlanckOstwaldSecond-2012.pdf).
- Ostwald never conceded the point; even in reviewing Planck's *Lectures on Thermodynamics* (1897), he maintained that only the equality $dS = 0$ had ever been used in applications of the Second Law (source: Deltete-PlanckOstwaldSecond-2012.pdf).

## Methods

Deltete's method is philosophical and historical: he works from Planck's published papers (1879–1897), Ostwald's published energetic studies and reviews, and especially the largely unexplored scientific correspondence between Planck and Ostwald (published in Ostwald 1961 and 1969). The essay traces the development of each man's position chronologically, reconstructs specific letter exchanges, and evaluates the philosophical cogency of arguments on both sides. Deltete brings contemporary philosophy-of-science categories (state functions vs path-dependent quantities; the logic of reduction) to bear on the historical debate. The essay is explicitly evaluative, not merely descriptive: Deltete argues that Planck was right and explains why.

## Limitations

- The essay focuses tightly on the Planck–Ostwald exchange and does not give equal treatment to the parallel dispute between Boltzmann and Ostwald/Helm, which was in many ways the more public and consequential confrontation.
- Georg Helm's more sophisticated version of energetics is acknowledged but largely set aside; Deltete notes Planck may have been unfair in lumping Helm and Ostwald together in his 1896 polemic.
- The essay does not discuss how Planck's own views subsequently evolved in response to quantum mechanics and Boltzmann's statistical interpretation—particularly Planck's eventual reluctant acceptance of statistical entropy after 1900.
- Ostwald's reasons for his position remain "impossible to isolate with any precision" (Deltete's own admission); the philosophical reconstruction of Ostwald's argument is necessarily incomplete.

## Connections

- [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]] — the interpretive core of the entire dispute; Planck insists it is the principle of irreversible entropy increase, Ostwald that it is limited to heat behaviour.
- [[entropy]] — Planck's universal quantitative measure of irreversibility; Ostwald restricts it to the capacity factor of heat energy in isothermal changes.
- [[thermodynamics]] — Planck aspires to make it a universal science of all natural processes; Ostwald subordinates it to energetics.
- [[reversible-cycle]] — the reversibility/irreversibility distinction is the axis of the entire debate; Planck argues it is fundamental, Ostwald that it is peripheral.
- [[carnot-cycle]] — Carnot's and Clausius's results on heat engines form the starting point from which both Planck and Ostwald depart; the impossibility of perpetual motion of the second kind is Planck's preferred formulation of the Second Law.
- [[caloric-theory]] — Carnot's original derivations assumed caloric; the 1850 reconciliation by Clausius (rejecting caloric while preserving Carnot's results) is the foundation for both Planck's and Ostwald's thermodynamics.
- [[planck-treatise-on-thermodynamics]] — Planck's *Lectures on Thermodynamics* (1897) is one of the key primary texts discussed; this source page is a direct complement to the treatise page.
- [[gibbs-elementary-principles-statistical-mechanics]] — Gibbs's thermodynamic formalism, including entropy as a state function and equilibrium as entropy maximum, is cited by Planck as a model for proper thermodynamic reasoning; Ostwald translated Gibbs's work into German (1893).

## Quotes

> "The significance of the second law, in its most general form, is to fix mathematically the sense of this direction in which all the transformations of nature take place." (Planck, 1879, quoted p. 124)

> "It is the foundation on the physical fact of irreversibility which gives strength to the second law. For if it must be admitted that a single exception would render the law completely untenable, it must also be admitted that any confirmation of a part supports the whole structure, imparting to conclusions even in apparently remote areas the full significance possessed by the law itself." (Planck, 1893, quoted p. 134)

> "The nerve of the proof [of the second law] is therefore not the inequality—whose non-occurrence is in fact being proved—but reversibility, and this holds true only for $dS = 0$. … The important fact that for most natural processes $dS > 0$ … has never been used in applications of that law." (Ostwald to Planck, July 9, 1893, quoted p. 138)
