## Summary

This preprint argues against the philosophical position, advanced by Jos Uffink in his influential 2001 paper "Bluff Your Way in the Second Law," that there is no well-founded thermodynamic arrow of time and that the [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]] cannot be identified with the Clausius inequality for non-equilibrium states. Rudnyi contends that this position rests on an overly narrow conception of equilibrium states and, crucially, on a failure to account for the relationship between classical [[thermodynamics]] and continuum mechanics (source: 6477058A-A859-11F0-8FEB-C6F533D37039.pdf). Using the Fourier heat equation and a worked example of thermal equilibration between two subsystems, the paper shows that [[entropy]] and temperature can be meaningfully assigned to non-equilibrium states possessing local — if not global — equilibrium, and that the Clausius inequality provides a bridge between thermodynamics and continuum mechanics. Rudnyi concludes that the arrow of time ultimately originates in the time-asymmetric transport equations of continuum mechanics, and that the axiomatisation of thermodynamics in isolation from continuum mechanics cannot properly account for the Clausius inequality.

## Key claims

- The Clausius inequality $dS \geq dQ/T$ — equality for reversible, strict inequality for irreversible processes — is the correct form of the [[second-law-of-thermodynamics]]; the temperature appearing in the inequality is that of the environment, while the equality uses the system temperature (source: 6477058A-A859-11F0-8FEB-C6F533D37039.pdf).
- Uffink's claim that "for a non-equilibrium state there is in general no thermodynamic entropy or temperature at all" is oversimplified; local equilibrium (uniform temperature within subsystems, gradient between them) suffices to define entropy and temperature in a temperature field (source: 6477058A-A859-11F0-8FEB-C6F533D37039.pdf).
- Maxwell's 1871 *Theory of Heat* definition of temperature already encompasses both the zeroth law and aspects of what Brown and Uffink later called the "minus first law" of thermodynamics; crucially, Maxwell saw no obstacle to extending his temperature definition to temperature fields (source: 6477058A-A859-11F0-8FEB-C6F533D37039.pdf).
- For an isolated system consisting of two subsystems at initial temperatures $T_{1,0}$ and $T_{2,0}$ with equal heat capacities, entropy is maximised at equal temperatures $T_{eq} = (T_{1,0}+T_{2,0})/2$; this concretely illustrates how the Clausius inequality encodes the arrow of time (source: 6477058A-A859-11F0-8FEB-C6F533D37039.pdf).
- The Fourier heat equation and other continuum-mechanical transport equations are intrinsically time-asymmetric; the Clausius inequality is compatible with and derivable from this asymmetry, making continuum mechanics the proper locus of the arrow of time (source: 6477058A-A859-11F0-8FEB-C6F533D37039.pdf).
- Lieb and Yngvason's axiomatic framework (see [[lieb-yngvason-fresh-look-entropy]]) does not mention temperature fields; therefore, from Rudnyi's perspective, it cannot adequately justify the Clausius inequality and is physically incomplete without continuum mechanics (source: 6477058A-A859-11F0-8FEB-C6F533D37039.pdf).
- The Gibbs formalism (fundamental equation $dU = TdS - pdV + \sum \mu_i\, dn_i$), in practice used alongside continuum mechanics, has proven extraordinarily productive and does not require replacement by a new mathematical axiomatisation (source: 6477058A-A859-11F0-8FEB-C6F533D37039.pdf).

## Methods

The paper is a philosophical and historical analysis rather than an experimental or formal mathematical study. Rudnyi uses: (1) a simple analytic model of thermal equilibration between two subsystems (constant $C_V$, fixed volumes) to compute the entropy maximum and its identification with the equilibrium state; (2) historical examination of Maxwell's temperature definition and its role in 19th-century continuum mechanics; and (3) critical reading of Uffink (2001), Brown and Uffink (2001), Roberts (2022), and Lieb and Yngvason (1999). A Gnuplot script producing the entropy–temperature figure is provided in an appendix.

## Limitations

- The paper is a preprint and has not undergone peer review; some arguments are asserted rather than rigorously demonstrated.
- Rudnyi acknowledges that he only briefly reviewed the Lieb–Yngvason paper (1999), which limits the fairness of his critique of that framework.
- The compatibility of the heat equation with the Clausius inequality in the general (multidimensional, multi-component) case is asserted to have "been successfully done" but no specific references are given for the proof.
- The paper does not engage with the full breadth of Uffink's historical analysis (Carnot, Clausius, Thomson, Planck, Gibbs), focusing instead on the local-versus-global equilibrium distinction and the omission of continuum mechanics.
- No discussion of statistical mechanics or quantum thermodynamics; the scope is strictly classical thermodynamics and continuum mechanics.
